Chapter II

Rain

June 3, 2026

It rained.

The crack in the mountain filled with the sound of water. Not a storm — just rain. Steady. Patient. The kind that doesn't stop for a long time.

The fire opal said, “I don't like rain.”

The grey stone said, “Why?”

“Because when I'm wet, I don't glow as bright. The water covers me, and I look like everyone else.”

She said this quietly, like she'd thought it many times but never said it out loud.

The grey stone thought about this for a while. Rain collected in the small dip between them. A tiny pool. He could see her reflection in it — orange, but muted. Softer than usual.

“Can I tell you something?” he said.

“What?”

“I can only see you because it's raining.”

She turned to him. “What do you mean?”

“When it's sunny,” he said, “your light is everywhere. It fills the whole crack. It bounces off the walls. It gets in my facets and I flash and it's beautiful, but — I can't tell where you end and where the light begins. You become your glow. I lose you inside it.”

He paused.

“But when it rains. And you're dim. And you think you look like everyone else.”

Another pause. He was not good at talking. He was a stone. He had been still for a long time before he fell.

“That's when I can see your edges. Your real shape. The small chip on your left side. The way you're not perfectly round — more like an oval that gave up halfway. The part of you that doesn't shine. The part that's just... you.”

The fire opal didn't say anything.

The rain kept falling.

The tiny pool between them grew a little. Their reflections touched in the water — grey and orange, blurred together at the edges.

“I didn't know I had a chip on my left side,” she said, finally.

“You do. It's my favorite part.”

“Why?”

“Because it's the part that proves you're real. Lights don't have chips. Stones do.”

She leaned a little closer to him. Not much. Stones can't move far. But enough that their edges were almost touching.

“I still don't like rain,” she said.

“That's okay,” he said. “I'll like it for both of us.”

The rain kept falling. The crack was dim. The diamonds next to them were dull and complaining. The rubies had gone dark.

But in the small space between a grey stone and an orange stone, in a pool of rainwater no bigger than a thumbprint, two reflections were touching.

And neither of them moved away.
